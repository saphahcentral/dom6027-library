“Amen”: Origins, Myths, and Misattributions

DOM6027 Research Library Article

Introduction

The expression “Amen” has long been used across many faiths to signify agreement, affirmation, or closure to prayer. However, some claim that the term has pagan origins, particularly in ancient Egypt with the deity Amun-Ra. This article examines these claims, linguistic roots, and scriptural evidence to determine whether such connections hold weight.

The Egyptian Deity Amun-Ra

Amun (also written Ammon or Amon) was a major deity in Egyptian religion, often merged with the sun god Ra to form Amun-Ra. Worship of Amun spread widely throughout North Africa and parts of the Near East. Some scholars and theologians have noted superficial phonetic similarities between Amun and Amen, leading to the assumption of direct etymological borrowing.

Hebrew Linguistic Evidence

In contrast to Egyptian origins, the Hebrew word אָמֵן (amen) derives from the root אָמַן (aman), meaning “to confirm,” “to support,” or “to be faithful.” This root appears throughout Scripture in forms such as emunah (faith or faithfulness). Thus, amen naturally conveys affirmation — “so be it,” “truly,” or “it is established.”

"Blessed be YHVH Elohim of Yisra’el from everlasting to everlasting: and let all the people say, Amen. Praise YHVH." - Tehillim 106:48

This verse clearly shows that the term amen existed within Hebrew worship and Scripture independent of Egyptian influence. The use of amen as an affirmation predates Greco-Roman religious syncretism and is rooted in YHVH’s covenantal faithfulness.

Comparative Analysis

Although linguistic parallels between Amun and Amen have been popularized, no credible linguistic evidence supports a direct derivation. Egyptian Amun is written with different consonants and meaning (“hidden one”), while Hebrew amen belongs to a Semitic root found across cognate languages (Aramaic, Arabic, Akkadian). The similarity in sound is therefore coincidental, not historical.

Moreover, ancient Israelites were explicitly warned against adopting the names of other deities (Shemot 23:13), which further invalidates the idea of an intentional borrowing into worship practices dedicated to YHVH.

Faithful Usage in Scripture

Yahusha Himself used amen repeatedly — often twice for emphasis (“Amen, amen, I say to you…”), which in Greek translations appears as ἀμήν (amēn). The consistent pattern from Torah through the Brit Chadashah demonstrates the word’s integrity as a term of truth, faithfulness, and divine assurance, not idolatry.

"For all the promises of Elohim in Him are Yea, and in Him Amen, to the glory of Elohim by us." - 2 Corinthians 1:20

Conclusion

The misconception that amen originated from the Egyptian god Amun-Ra arises primarily from surface-level phonetic similarity. Scriptural linguistics, however, trace amen to a pure Hebrew root affirming truth and steadfastness in YHVH. To reject the word on the basis of coincidental sound patterns is unnecessary and historically unfounded. The faithful may continue to affirm prayers in confidence, declaring their agreement with the will and word of YHVH.

Baruch YHVH.
